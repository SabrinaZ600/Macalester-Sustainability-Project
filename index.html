The zero‑waste goal

In 2011, Macalester set a goal to reach zero‑waste by 2020 — defined as diverting 90% of waste away from landfills and incinerators.

Long story short, we didn't make it there.

How are we doing?

Diversion rates from 2020 to 2025

Diversion rate is caclulated as the weight of re-directed material (recycling + compost) divided by total waste (trash + recycling + compost).

Diversion rates have hovered around 60~80%, but they have largely stagnated around 70% in the past five years.

The dashed line shows a simple linear regression trend over time.

Diversion rates aren't the full story

Diversion only tells you where material in the the waste bins go — not whether we’re producing less or more waste on the whole, nor how accurately it is sorted.

This stacked view shows total pounds by quarter, broken down by waste stream. Data from the past two years show seasonal variation, and a decrease in generate waste trends overall.

However, the proportion of landfill-bound trash to other waste streams hasn't changed in this time.

How Accurately are Students Sorting Their Waste?

Let’s start with a baseline: how well are students sorting waste when there’s no competition, no feedback, and no extra attention?

Baseline sorting accuracy (Fall 2025)

This chart shows waste audit data from September 2025, collected during a period with no sorting competition or targeted intervention. Overall accuracy is quite low, with large amounts of material ending up in the wrong bins. Common issues include plastic bags, food waste left in packaging, and confusion between recycling and trash.

Darker bars represent correctly sorted material; lighter bars show mis‑sorted material.

What happens when people pay attention?

In Fall 2024, first‑year dorms participated in a Green Dorm sorting competition. With clear expectations and feedback, sorting accuracy improved week‑over‑week.

So, how are we doing?

We've got work to do, and you can help!

If the goal is a zero‑waste & sustainable future, diversion can’t be the only metric — Measuring zero-waste progress involves more than that. We can see that while our waste output seems to be lowering, there is much room for improvement sorting waste - we still have a high proportion of mis-sorted trash. Get closer to zero-waste each day by reducing what you bring in, sorting conscientiously, and using the right drop‑offs for hard‑to‑recycle items.

Ways you can be sustainable

  • Use the map to find the right drop‑off for tricky items (batteries, e‑waste, film plastics, etc.).
  • Don’t “wishcycle" and contribute to contaimination - When in doubt throw it out
  • Reduce waste upstream: reuse containers/mugs, avoid single‑use items, and choose refill/return options when possible.
